carekit

Carekit is a mobile application that promotes self-compassion through the creation of a interactive compassionate kit.

YEARS

2020

ROLES

Research, User Interface Design, Branding, User Testing

While offering compassion to others in times of need comes quite naturally for most, treating yourself with the same kindness, concern and support you’d show to a good friend doesn't always come as easily. The practice of self-compassion being particularily necessary in this time of the COVID-19 pandemic, I wanted to create an interactive mobile platform that could be used to create ones very own compassionate kit bag, a tool proposed in compassion-focused Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT).

Some of the 'treasures' I illustrated for the app.

Simply put, a compassionate kit is a place to put reminders for your compassionate self, so you can use your resources to nourish you along life's journey. These kit bags usually take on a physical form like a bag, box or other type of container, and are meant to be kept in a place its user can access everyday.

More 'treasures' I illustrated for the app.

Taking inspiration from the questions asked by Kristin Neff's Self-Compassion Scale, I developed a short, 5 min. Google survey which I shared amongst close friends and family and their acquaintances. Respondents were asked to imagine a compassionate image and share with me their idea of their ideal safe place. They were then provided with a list of items and asked to select the ones they would add to their kit if they had one, with the option to add their own ideas of items. Overall, the survey was designed to allow for complete anonymity.

A collage of images inspired by the respondents' answers to their ideal compassionate images and safe places. Photos by Unsplash or provided by the respondents.

After closing the survey, I compiled the responses in a spreadsheet and mapped out some of the ideas for the compassionate image and for the safe place in a moodboard. Using a word cloud generator, I also identified the most used words given by respondents. The most used words or the compassionate image included tree, warm, hug and sky. For the safe place, the most used words included ocean, beach, bed and forest.

A wordcloud I generated using the most popular words used by respondents on their ideal compassionate image.

Following this, I developed a user flow chart to identify the flow of the app, from the creation of the account to the main home interface. It was important for me to start with the most popular items chosen in the survey.

In my first iteration of the home interface, I imagined the kit as a backpack sitting in a safe place, which the user could open up to remind them of their compassionate selves. However, as I continued to work on it, it became apparent that the interaction should be with the items inside the kit, and not the kit itself. With this new lead, I began developing templates for safe places in which the items could be displayed: ocean, forest, bedroom and sky. I named the app carekit and developed a logo and colour palette for the interface.

With the interface complete, I then animated short interactions of the interface using ProtoPie and contacted respondents that expressed interest in participating in user testing. I asked participants to provide me with a photo of a happy moment that I could use to personalize their experience. Using Zoom, I met with 6 participants remotely over the span of 3 days. Sharing their screen with me, I was able to follow along as each participant walked through their experience of using the app. Each session took an average of 60 minutes.
